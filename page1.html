Part I: How Medicine is Used as a Political Weapon in Times of Conflict

Case 1: Medicine as a Means for Colonialism — Algerian War of Independence

The Algerian War of Independence (1954—1962) between French colonial forces and the National Liberation Front (FLN) — which was the nationalist political party leading the movement for independence— does not at first glance indicate any relation between the two opposing forces in their political discourse and the use of medicine. Many accounts have depicted this conflict as a strictly military affair, wrought with physical violence and political strategies from both sides to attain power.

Image Credits:https://www.nytimes.com/2018/10/04/opinion/france-torture-algeria-war.html

Question

Successful Colonization Tactics involve:

  • A) Use of military to physically attain control over a region
  • B) Economic strategies to exploit the region
  • C) providing medical care to the people residing in the area to win their support
  • D) All of the above
  • E) A and B only
Answer: D) All of the Above

Research that involved acquiring information from Algerian sources, demonstrates that “the fight between the two sides was not simply a military affair; it also involved diverse and competing claims about how was positioned to better care for the Algerian people’s health and welfare.”6

Winning the support of the residing population is essential in the fight for sovereignty, and both sides understood that demonstrations of military power through wars and physical violence can only do so much good before causing harm in this fight. In order to counterbalance the terror and stress inflicted on the residing population from the military violence, the combating forces needed to somehow show the people that they also are capable of caring for them, protecting them, and safeguarding their health. This was ultimately done through abstracting medical practice to serve their own political agendas in their war efforts. The nationalist leadership constructed national health care-institutions that provided care for the population to demonstrate that they were able to effectively care for their own people. This ultimately contributed to obtaining international recognition of Algerian sovereignty.6

Despite losing the war, the French also used medicine and health care to try to secure the support of the population through the establishment of the Sections Administratives Spécialisées (SAS) — “the most comprehensive wartime initiative, aimed to facilitate political rapprochement between the Algerian population and the colonial state.”6 This initiative involved sending physicians, nurses, and other medical personnel as political ambassadors to rural areas to provide free care, and ultimately to “conquer with medicine rather than bullets.”

Takeaways

In this way, medicine not only in the context of Algeria but around the world, can serve political purposes whether intended or not. The care that people receive from their medical practitioners forms their ideas of how reliable and effective the political institutions leading their country are.

This inevitable relation that links medical practitioners to politics makes it hard for the medical field to remain apolitical, and necessitates that practitioners have a strong awareness of the political environment in which they practice.

Case 2: Weaponizing Medicine to Cripple a Nation — U.S. Occupation of Iraq

The idea of compromising the ethics of medicine by politicizing its usage is not foreign to U.S. military strategists. Under the Bush Administration, laws were established during the Global War on Terror to allow privately held corporations to “rebuild” Iraq and Afghanistan in ways that undermined the health and nutrition of local populations. This was done through the establishment of the Coalition Provisional Authority (CPA)— a transitional government in Iraq— which privatized the previously state-run Iraqi health care system.13

This directly affected medical practitioners in Iraq and the care they were able to provide as it banned thousands of Iraqi physicians and nurses from public-sector employment. As the U.S. invasion in Iraq evolved into long-term occupation, the sectarian political system created by the CPA was met with opposition from the Iraqi population, which inevitably caused Iraqi physicians who were working within the CPA’s new health system to be targeted for violence. This use of healthcare to accomplish a political agenda ultimately “contributed to the chaotic conditions that helped plunge Iraq into an acute-on-chronic public health emergency, which it still remains in today.”13

Medicine has been weaponized in the history of conflict between the U.S. and Iraq in even more subtler ways.

Question

Who is currently the biggest investor in regenerative medicine?

  • A) Big Pharma Companies
  • B) U.S. Military
  • C) UK Regenerative Medicine Platform
  • D) Stem Cell Agencies
Answer: B) U.S. Military

The U.S. military is so far the biggest investor in the field of regenerative medicine, which was “formally recognized by the military when it established the Armed Forces Institute of Regenerative Medicine in 2008.”13

Throughout the American wars waged in Iraq and Afghanistan, biomedicine and the ethics of care of medical practice have been used to obscure the root causes and effects of violence from war. For example, there is a "destroy-and-build logic" whereby science is used to create novel, and more damaging explosives for warfare, and medicine is used as a tool to salvage new forms of wounds and trauma to allow for continued violence.13

Question

Explosion-related injuries entail:

  • A) radiation poisoning
  • B) asphyxiation
  • C) thermal burns
  • D) renal failure from damaged tissues releasing chemicals
  • E) All of the above
Answer: E) All of the above

While inarguably complicated, these devastating cases of physical trauma from explosives can now be survived due to advancements in blood-clotting products, rapid emergency evacuation procedures, and other biomedical improvements. However, these “improvements” in medicine disguise the need to end the escalating violence created by warfare that contradicts the purpose of medicine and the ethics of care in the first place.

Takeaways
  1. By involving health and medicine in military strategies, nations can be severely crippled as the health of a population is essential to ensuring the stability of a nation.
  2. Furthermore, despite efforts to keep hospitals and places of medical care politically neutral, political conflict will still enter the medical sphere, as shown in the example of Iraqi physicians working during the time of U.S. occupation. For this reason, it is important that physicians remain aware of the political environment in which they are serving patients.

Case 3: Medicine as a Tool for Counterinsurgency — U.S. Tactics in Pakistan

Another key example of medicine being weaponized is in the usage of medicine under the Obama Administration in Pakistan. Many recall the military operation that led to the assassination of Osama bin Laden in the context of the 40 minute-long military mission, during which U.S. special forces were able to raid an al-Qaeda compound, find, and kill bin Laden. However, this narrative neglects the importance of the use of medicine to achieve what today is praised as a “flawless mission.” Interestingly, the military operation first began through the U.S. government using sham vaccination campaigns to gather intelligence on bin Laden’s DNA and location.13

Question

As a result of the misuse of vaccination campaigns to murder bin Laden, cases of Polio in Pakistan increased by __________ between 2013 and 2014:

  • A) 30%
  • B) did not increase at all
  • C) 50%
  • D) 60%
Answer: D) 60%

This exploitation and corruption of medicine with the ulterior motives of gaining military intelligence for an assassination expectedly created long-lasting distrust in medical practitioners and even “backlash against immunization in Pakistan,” leading to an increase in cases of polio.13

Takeaways
  1. The intersection of medicine and politics makes it such that medicine (which is governed by the ethics of care) can be misused in political conflicts of war (which is governed by the ethics of violence).
  2. Political conflict can make it such that rather than working out of good will and under the ethics of medicine, medical personnel can actually be working towards obscured motives that can ultimately harm the health of the population they intended to serve. In this way, it is necessary that medical practitioners be politically engaged and use their voices as citizens to call-out their governments for such exploitations and corruptions of medicine.
