Part II. How Political Conflict Affects and Changes Medical Practice

From Part I, we’ve seen how the weaponization of medicine in political affairs is a direct consequence of political and military strategists recognizing the power of medicine as it can shape the care a population receives for better or for worse. In these examples, we’ve seen how medicine has entered the political sphere. Nonetheless, there are also many examples of politics entering the medical sphere and shaping medical practice, which we will look at in Part II of this training module through 3 selected cases.

Case 1: Remodeling Medicine in Third Reich

During the early decades of the 20th century, the growth of the eugenics movement, which was based on the belief that human progress could be snared on through national breeding programs designed to increase the number of children born to the upper-classes and to discourage the expansion of the lower-classes, began to take speed. This evolved into the Nazi ideology that caused the suffering and deaths of Jews and other marginalized groups at the expense of pursuing a “pure,” Aryan race.

The political environment created from this ideology inevitably corrupted the practice of medicine in Germany. As summarized in Michael Kater’s Doctors Under Hitler, physicians who were “trained to care for the sick, relieve suffering, and save lives” began withholding care, inflicting pain, unethically experimenting on human subjects, and committing murder. Physicians from the time of Nazi Germany first-handedly expressed this shift in the professional ethos of medicine, declaring statements such as “because I wish to be a physician, I have to be a National Socialist…the Nazi worldview and physicianship condition complement each other.” The political environment left little room for opposition, leading many physicians to adopt the notion of viewing Jews and others as “a gangrenous appendix” that needs to be removed from the “diseased body” of the population of Germany in order to fulfill their ethical obligations as doctors to “preserve life.”

Many physicians began to try to find connections between their work in medicine and Nazi ideology, leading to cases of malpractice and unethical research. One particularly compelling case of unethical research is discussed in Hidelbrandt’s The Anatomy of Murder, which gives insight into the ways in which the work of medical practitioners and researchers alike shifted to prove the circulating racist ideology and to use the “material” of prisoners’ bodies offered unreservedly by the government. Eduard Pernkopf was a famed anatomist who made use of the bodies of executioned, political prisoners to construct Pernkopf’s Atlas on the Topographical Anatomy of Man. While today many have criticized this atlas, it is hard to reject Pernkopf’s data as even in 2019, it is still considered unsurpassed in comprehensives and precision than any other anatomy book. In addition to this atlas, many other commonly used tools and pools of knowledge used in pre-medical and medical science courses have been drawn from such unethically sound research and are still used today in medical practice.

Question

How should such medical materials that have been derived unethically from the bodies of executioned, Jewish prisoners be handled today?

  • A) if it will save a life, it should be used
  • B) it should be rejected all together to show a firm level of intolerance
  • C) If used, it should be used with respect, gratitude, and appropriate solemnity in remembrance of the victims exploited for the material
  • D) A and C
Answer: D) A and C

Jewish religious leaders have stated that while they are deeply saddened that German scientists and physicians have exploited their people under the guise of medical advancement, they still believe that if in any case the material produced can save someone’s life, it should be used. However, they do feel strongly that the usage needs to involve respect for those who have suffered and a remembrance for the victims so as to curb the repeating of history in future cases.

Application

Despite the U.S. trying to distance themselves for the criminal medical practices that occurred in Germany, the U.S. has its own past of allowing political discourse to corrupt research and medical practice.For example, the Tuskegee Syphilis study conducted with government support between 1932 and 1972 was laden with racist ideology that justified withholding penicillin from the 600 black men suffering from syphilis in hopes of justifying fundamentally unequal treatment programs for African-Americans.

This deception that was condoned by the political climate continues to have lasting effects on medical practice in America, as it has ultimately hurt physician-patient relationships between minority patients and non-minority doctors.
Takeaway
  • Given the long-lasting consequences of unethical medical practice can have on future practice around the world, physicians must be aware of how the political climate can affect their practice.
  • Political awareness allows physicians to denounce the actions of other doctors, not only within their country but around the world, who under the influence of politics begin to misuse medicine. Without such political awareness to see political roots of unethical medical practice, we are unable to better prevent such heinous acts in the history of medicine from occurring again.

Case 2: Conflict in Kashmir Infiltrating Medicine

The issue of sectarian violence in the region of Kashmir quickly became an issue within what was intended to be a politically-neutral hospital. Beginning in January of 2005, the District Headquarter Hospital (DHQ) in Kashmir that was staffed by Shias, Sunnis, and Ismalis became a “space for political unrest, religious protests, and acts of terrorism,” as the Sunni-Shia conflict in the region infiltrated the hospital (Varley 2016).

According to Varley, the Sunni-Shia conflicts made it nearly impossible for medical practitioners to remain neutral, leading to the development of “sectarian logics of care” within the DHQ, whereby providers began to administer care in biased ways that aligned with the sect that they supported. This, accordingly, led to persistent fear and distrust from patients in not knowing whether or not the actions of medical staff were being influenced by Sunni or Shia ideologies. Patients and doctors alike felt it was “impossible to be healthy in Kashmir” due to the ongoing tensions, leading to widespread trauma and PTSD within the population.

In some accounts, too little government intervention is to blame for the ways in which medicine was corrupted by the political environment. Interlocutors were cited saying that the government did not do enough to ensure the security of patients and medical personnel or to prevent the sectarian violence that resulted in the massacre of patients and providers in the DHQ (Varley 2016). Other accounts, however, criticize the Indian state for having too much intervention in the humanitarian efforts thereafter that tried to remedy the widespread trauma through therapy programs and mental health initiatives.

Question

Throughout the 1990s before the height of the conflict in Kashmir, Indian military officials responded to human rights reports by:

  • A) undermining their validity
  • B) supporting them
  • C) with indifference
  • D) None of the above
Answer: A) undermining their validity

Prior to the height of the conflict in Kashmir, human rights reports and medical evidence that showed a need for mental health initiatives and therapy for the population where deemed “worthless,” and in some instances “antinational.”

However, upon recognizing the power of medicine to gain the support of the Kashmiri during the conflict, the government began using the people’s trauma and PTSD to make political claims, and even co-opted the trauma therapy programs launched by MSF as alternatives to political solutions (Varma 2018). Using these therapy programs, the Indian state attempted to launch its new “healing policy” to win the hearts of those who lived in Indian-occupied Kashmir. Such corrupted motives on behalf of the government negatively affects reception of programs intended for enhancing health.
Takeaway
  • While there are cases in which excessive government intervention has been shown to corrupt medicine, the case study in Kashmir shows that some level of government interaction in medicine is needed to provide security to the care being provided in medical institutions.
  • For this reason, it is important for physicians and medical personnel to be part of government discussions regarding medicine so as allow for a reasonable balance of government intervention in medicine.

Case 3: Political Sentiments Shaping Medicine During the American Civil War

The American Civil War was a particularly politically-charged period in the history of the United States as it was fought over the controversy of economics and morality of the enslavement of black people and states’ rights. Today, the Civil War is regarded America’s deadliest war, as 500,000 military personnel died, which is almost half of all Americans who have ever died during wartime

Question

During the American Civil War, soldiers’ deaths and suffering stemmed mainly from:

  • A) Traumatic, battle-field related injuries
  • B) Non-traumatic cases
  • C) Equally of both
Answer: B) Non-traumatic cases

According to Michael Flannery in his work titled Civil War Pharmacy, the major causes of death are not from the dangers of the battle-field but were from the dangers present within the camps, and the limitations on medical care to safeguard the health of soldiers against these dangers. “The average soldier was about twice as likely to die of a disease contracted in the camp than of a wound acquired in battle,” (22).

However, despite the shared environments that both black and white troops had to endure, their mortality outcomes were starkly different, and this was due to the medical care provided. Among black troops, there were 892 cases of camp fevers per 1,000, 839 cases of diarrhea/dysentery per 1,000, and 354 cases of respiratory ailments per 1000. “In each category, incidents of illness appreciably exceeded that for white roots, suggesting that medical care in camp and field was substandard compared with that provided for whites,” (23).

Political sentiments from the slavery-era affected how physicians provided care to troops based on their rase. As Flannery recount, “Military officials and doctors made a number of claims about black people’s physiology, from arguments that black men were innately invulnerable to fever and thereby better suited to be stationed in the swampy climes of Louisiana to the false notion that black men fell victim to dysentery because of their dietary habits of slavery.” In this way, we see how the political climate can influence doctors in how they administer treatment and justify these different forms of care.

Question

In 2017, what fraction of black patients cite experiencing discrimination in health care?

  • A) 1 in 10
  • B) 1 in 5
  • C) 5 in 10
  • D) 2 in 5
Answer: B) 1 in 5

Studies from the University of California— San Francisco indicate that even 150 year since the American Civil War, discrimination in health care is still a large problem. Interestingly, “blacks’ reports of discrimination actually increase as wealth increases,” suggesting that a significant number of cases of healthcare discrimination go unreported.

Application

Today, the political discourse in our country on how we view immigrants, minorities, or other marginalized groups can shape how we practice medicine.

A study on health discrimination in physicians’ diagnoses was conducted by assessing 720 physicians at 2 national meetings of organization of primary care physicians. The assessment involved having the physician watch recorded interviews about hypothetical patients, and then make recommendations for the patients. The actors in the recorded interviews presented the same symptoms, and only differed in whether or not they were male, female, black, or white. The outcome of this test was that race and sex of a patient influence how physicians manage chest pain. For example, black women were significantly less likely to be referred for catheterization than white men, with an odds ratio of 0.4.

Takeaway

The long-standing history of how political discourse as affected medicine and continues to influence physicians’ decisions when administering care verifies that physicians cannot practice medicine in a vacuum. Rather, physicians are going to be influenced by the political environment around them. Thus it is important that doctors have an awareness of the context in which they practice and how that shapes the biases they may form in order to progress past current issues of health discrimination.
